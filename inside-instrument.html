The ten major musical instruments in ancient China generally refer to pipa, erhu, Bianzhong, Xiao, Di, Se, Qin, Cuan, Sheng and drum. Here we mainly talk about pipa, erhu, Cuan, Di, Qin, Zheng.

Chinese classical musical instruments are generally classified according to "eight tones". "Bayin" is the earliest method to classify musical instruments according to the manufacturing materials of musical instruments in China. It can be seen in the spring palace of Zhouli, which can be divided into eight categories: gold, stone, earth, leather, silk, wood, Pao and bamboo "Gold" category: clock; Soil: Xun; "Leather" category: drum; "Silk" category: Qin, Se, erhu, pipa, etc; "Pao" category: Sheng; Bamboo: Xiao, flute, etc.

Qin was invented in the Fuxi era. It is a five stringed Qin, or it was written by Yun for Shennong (2370 bc-2338 BC). "Gangjian yizhilu": Fuxi's zhuotong was the Qin, the rope was the string, and guansang was the harp. Shuo Wen: Qin, the string music by Bao Xi. Emperor's century: Shennong began to make a five string zither with the sound of Gongshang horn. From the ninth Dynasty to King Wen, the second string was added, which was called Shaogong and Shaoshang Qin was invented in western Shandong and Eastern Henan (the capital of Fuxi is in Huaiyang, Henan, and the capital of Shennong is in Qufu, Shandong).

In ancient times, Cuan was a kind of wind instrument made of clay. It was round or oval and had six holes. Cuan originated from the labor and production activities of the Han ancestors. At first, it may be made by the ancestors to imitate the calls of birds and animals, so as to trap prey. Later, with the progress of society, it evolved into a simple musical instrument, and gradually increased the number of musical holes, and developed into a melodic musical instrument that can play tunes.

Flute is one of the most distinctive wind instruments in China. In May 1986, 16 vertical bone Flutes (made from the bones of birds) were excavated from the early Neolithic site in Jiahu village, Wuyang County, Henan Province. According to the measurement, they have a history of more than 8000 years. In vertical blowing, the sound holes range from five to eight, most of which are seven hole flutes, which have roughly the same scale as the Chinese tradition we are familiar with.

Erhu, also known as "huqin", appeared in Tang Dynasty, known as "Xiqin" and Song Dynasty as "Jiqin". It is generally believed that today's huqin developed from Xiqin and has become a unique string instrument in China. It is not only suitable for the expression of deep, sad content, but also can describe the magnificent artistic conception. Erhu is one of the main bowstring instruments in the family of Chinese musical instruments. The word huqin appeared in the Tang Dynasty. At that time, the western and northern nationalities were called Hu people, and huqin was the general name of musical instruments introduced by Western and northern nationalities. After Yuan Dynasty and Ming and Qing Dynasties, huqin became the general name of string wiping instrument.

Pipa is known as "the king of plucked instruments", which belongs to plucked stringed instruments. It is made of wood. The speaker is half pear shaped with four strings on it. Originally it was made of silk thread, but now it is mostly made of steel wire, steel rope and nylon. The neck and panel are equipped with "phase" and "product" to determine the phoneme. It is an important national instrument for Solo, accompaniment, ensemble and ensemble

Guzheng, also known as Han Zheng, Qin Zheng, Yao Zheng and Luan Zheng, is one of the traditional Chinese musical instruments, belonging to plucked instruments. It is one of the unique and important national musical instruments in China. The structure of guzheng is composed of panel, string, front Yueshan, string nail, tuning box, foot, back Yueshan, side plate, sound outlet, bottom plate and stringing hole. The shape of Zheng is a rectangular wooden speaker. The string frame "Zheng column" (i.e. Yan column) can move freely, with one string and one tone. It is arranged according to the five tone scale. The earliest is 38 string Zheng (fense · Zheng). In Tang and Song Dynasties, there were 13 strings, and then increased to 16, 18, 21, 25 strings. The most commonly used specification is 21 strings
